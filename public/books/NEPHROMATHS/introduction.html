Chapter 1 Introduction

“Renal physiology has now passed into a quantitative phase where unsupported speculation and empirical description are no longer warranted.”

— Homer Smith, 1951

One of the great attractions of renal physiology is that is amenable to simple quantitative analysis. Long before we learnt of their molecular basis, the fundamental processes of the kidney were understood by Homer Smith and others in terms of simple quantitative models.

In learning these models, and their associated equations, the student of nephrology comes to understand the physical basis of the underlying physiology. For example, the fractional excretion ((2.2)) of a substance is a concept that considers glomerular filtration and tubular reabsorption as independent, sequential processes: as they are in reality. Moreover these equations can be used to diagnose electrolyte and acid-base disorders and to guide their clinical management.

However, the equations can be difficult to remember - as can the assumptions that underlie their validity. As a result, they are often not applied or worse mis-applied. Therefore this book aims to provide a quick-reference guide to clinicians wishing to use simple calculations to help diagnose and treat disorders in electrolyte and acid-base homeostasis.

This book does not aim to act as a guide to clinical management (for the reasons stated in the ??). Neither does this book aim to provide a comprehensive discussion of renal or fluid-electrolyte physiology; there are plenty of books, blogs and articles that already do this.

1.2 Help! - I’m in a hurry and don’t have time to read this properly…

When faced with any electrolyte or acid-base disorder without time to think it through properly, one can nearly always find the correct diagnosis by asking for a set of basic investigations and then returning to these when there is a bit of time to think.

Tests to request if in doubt

Send the following samples and think about it later:

  1. Blood for:
    • U&Es
    • HCO3 (sometimes called TCO2)
    • Cl–
    • glucose
    • albumin
    • osmolality
       
  2. A spot urine sample for:
    • Na+
    • K+
    • Cl–
    • osmolality
